## Speed is a constraint, not a virtue

A fast build that breaks in production isn't fast — it's a slower build with extra steps. When
we say we build fast, we mean the *decisions* move fast: scope gets locked in days, not weeks,
and we ship in reviewable increments instead of one big reveal at the end.

What we don't compress is the part that determines whether the thing still works in six months:
error handling, data modeling, and anything touching money or user data.

## What we refuse to skip

- **Type safety.** Every project ships in TypeScript, strict mode, no exceptions.
- **A real staging environment.** "It worked on my machine" isn't a launch plan.
- **Accessibility basics.** Keyboard navigation and screen reader support aren't a phase-two item.
- **A rollback plan.** If a deploy breaks something, we know how to undo it in minutes, not hours.

## Where the speed actually comes from

Most of it isn't about typing faster. It's about not re-deciding things we already decided —
which is the entire reason a design system exists before a single page gets built. Once the
tokens, motion language, and component primitives are settled, every new section is assembly,
not invention.

The second source of speed is scope discipline. We'd rather ship a smaller version of the right
thing than a complete version of the wrong thing. That's a conversation we have in week one, not
a surprise in week six.
